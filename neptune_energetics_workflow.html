Synthetic Hydropower Plant — process model
Synthetic Hydropower Plant — process model
A modular power plant that converts low quality thermal energy that would otherwise be rejected to the environment into electricity, in a power dense, low cost package.
High velocity gas Low velocity water High velocity liquid mixture High pressure liquid mixture Low pressure liquid mixture Energy input/output Low velocity refrigerant
1
Evaporation. Low exergy thermal energy from a data center cooling unit or power plant flue gas stack is used to vaporize a liquid refrigerant to convert it into a high velocity gas.
2
Mixing. The high velocity refrigerant gas is then injected into a low velocity water stream to increase the kinetic energy of the water by transferring the momentum of the refrigerant to the water through drag forces. This process also condenses the refrigerant.
3
Pressurization. The high velocity refrigerant/water mixture flows into a diffuser where its pressure is increased by decelerating the flow, which converts its kinetic energy into static pressure.
4
Power generation. The high pressure liquid mixture drives a hydroelectric turbine that produces electricity.
5
Separation. The fluid mixture is separated back into water and refrigerant streams. The refrigerant returns to absorb waste heat in the evaporator and the water returns to the injection stream to restart the cycle.

Comparative metrics
Low-grade heat source
Runs on heat others throw away
A 60 °C source evaporates the refrigerant at ~50 °C. Steam plants need a few hundred degrees; organic-Rankine machines lose most of their output as the source falls toward 100 °C. Data-center coolant, flue-gas tails and process return water sit exactly where conventional engines stop working.
Comparative power density
More kilowatts per cubic metre
The working fluid is liquid water carrying the energy, not low-density vapour. The plant delivers 10 kW/m³ and 25 kW/m² of footprint — several times the volumetric output of a comparable low-temperature heat engine.

Evaporation, mixing, pressurisation and separation all happen in static geometry — vessels, a nozzle and a diffuser with no moving surfaces. The only spinning component is the hydroelectric turbine-generator.

Low capital cost
Cheap because nothing is exotic
Low temperature and low speed remove the expensive parts of a heat engine. What is left is commodity hardware.
−
No high-temperature alloys — 50–60 °C operation allows plain steel and standard refrigerant-grade materials.
−
No high-speed vapour turbine or compressor — the kinetic step happens in a fixed nozzle.
−
Pressure is recovered in a passive diffuser, not precision turbomachinery.
+
Power is taken by a single off-the-shelf hydro turbine-generator, a mature, low-cost product.
